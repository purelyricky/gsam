\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have become foundational tools across nearly every domain of computing, from software engineering and scientific research to financial analysis and legal reasoning \citep{brown2020language, openai2023gpt4}. Yet despite their remarkable general capabilities, LLMs struggle with specialized domains that demand precise, structured, and evolving knowledge. A model trained on broad internet data does not inherently understand the hierarchical relationships within the XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) taxonomy used in financial reporting, nor can it reason about how an error in identifying one financial entity propagates through downstream formulas \citep{loukas2022finer}.

Two broad strategies have emerged to address this gap. \textit{Fine-tuning} updates the model's weights on domain-specific data \citep{wang2025finlora}, but is expensive, non-interpretable, and difficult to update incrementally. \textit{Context adaptation} (also called context engineering) instead modifies the \textit{inputs} to the model---injecting instructions, strategies, examples, or evidence into the prompt without altering weights \citep{ace2025}. Context adaptation is interpretable (the injected knowledge is readable text), cheap to update (no retraining), and shareable across models.

Recent context adaptation frameworks have shown impressive results. Dynamic Cheatsheet \citep{suzgun2025dynamic} introduced the idea of an evolving memory of strategies accumulated during test-time inference. ACE (Agentic Context Engineering) \citep{ace2025} addressed two critical failure modes of earlier approaches---\textit{brevity bias} (where optimization collapses toward overly concise summaries that lose domain detail) and \textit{context collapse} (where iterative LLM-driven rewriting gradually degrades accumulated knowledge)---through a multi-agent architecture with incremental delta updates. ACE achieved average gains of 8.6\% on financial benchmarks and 10.6\% on agent tasks, while reducing adaptation latency by 86.9\% compared to prior methods \citep{ace2025}.

However, both Dynamic Cheatsheet and ACE share a fundamental architectural limitation: they store accumulated knowledge as \textit{flat collections of bullet points}. Each bullet is an independent unit with metadata (helpful and harmful counters), but with no explicit connections to other bullets. This design discards a signal that is critically important in specialized domains: the \textit{relationships between knowledge elements}.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

In this thesis, I introduce \textbf{GSAM (Graph-Structured Adaptive Memory)}, a framework that replaces flat bullet-point storage with an ontology-grounded knowledge graph. In GSAM, domain concepts, strategies, anti-patterns, formulas, and their interrelationships are modeled as typed nodes and edges within a directed graph. The framework extends ACE's Generator--Reflector--Curator architecture with two new components: a \textbf{Graph Constructor} that builds and maintains the knowledge graph from learning experiences, and a \textbf{Graph Retriever} that extracts relevant subgraphs for new tasks through a combination of embedding similarity and structural graph traversal.

The research targets three hypotheses:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=1.5cm]
\item[\textbf{H1:}] Graph-structured memory with ontology grounding improves cross-concept knowledge transfer compared to flat storage.
\item[\textbf{H2:}] Explicit failure cascade modeling reduces repeated and downstream errors.
\item[\textbf{H3:}] Ontology-aware retrieval achieves higher precision than embedding-only retrieval.
\end{description}

I evaluate GSAM on two financial analysis benchmarks---FiNER \citep{loukas2022finer} (139-type entity recognition) and Formula \citep{wang2025finlora} (numerical reasoning over financial filings)---and additionally introduce \textbf{FiNER-Transfer}, a new benchmark specifically designed to measure cross-concept transfer within the XBRL taxonomy. The choice to focus exclusively on domain-specific financial tasks, rather than general agent benchmarks such as AppWorld \citep{trivedi2024appworld}, is deliberate: financial analysis provides formal ontological structure (the XBRL taxonomy), natural dependency graphs (formulas depend on entity extraction), and compositional error cascades---all of which are naturally captured by graphs but invisible in flat storage.

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the relevant literature on context adaptation, knowledge graphs, and financial NLP. \section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Large Language Models and Context Windows}

The transformer architecture \citep{vaswani2017attention} enabled a new paradigm in natural language processing by processing input sequences through self-attention mechanisms rather than recurrence. Scaling this architecture to billions of parameters produced models such as GPT-3 \citep{brown2020language}, GPT-4 \citep{openai2023gpt4}, LLaMA \citep{touvron2023llama}, and DeepSeek \citep{deepseek2024}, which exhibit strong few-shot learning capabilities across diverse tasks. The key insight underlying few-shot learning is that the model's behavior can be substantially altered by what appears in the \textit{context window}---the input provided alongside the query \citep{brown2020language}.

This observation motivated a shift from fine-tuning (modifying weights) toward \textit{context engineering} (modifying inputs) as a more flexible approach to domain adaptation. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) \citep{lewis2020retrieval} retrieves relevant documents to augment the context. Chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain} elicits step-by-step reasoning by structuring the context appropriately. Decomposed prompting \citep{khot2022decomposed} breaks complex tasks into modular sub-tasks. These methods demonstrate that \textit{what} is in the context matters as much as \textit{how} the model was trained.

\subsection{From Dynamic Cheatsheet to ACE}

The evolution from static prompts to evolving memory systems represents the current frontier of context engineering. \textbf{Dynamic Cheatsheet} \citep{suzgun2025dynamic} introduced the concept of a ``cheatsheet''---an external memory of reusable strategies that accumulates during test-time inference. As the model encounters new tasks, it reflects on its performance and adds successful strategies to the cheatsheet. This simple idea produced substantial improvements, but suffered from two problems. First, \textit{brevity bias}: the LLM tasked with maintaining the cheatsheet tended to compress entries into progressively shorter summaries, losing domain-specific detail. Second, \textit{context collapse}: iterative rewriting by the same LLM gradually degraded the quality of accumulated knowledge.

\textbf{ACE (Agentic Context Engineering)} \citep{ace2025} addressed both problems through architectural innovation. Rather than having a single model manage the entire memory, ACE separates responsibilities across three specialized agents:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item The \textbf{Generator} produces reasoning trajectories for new queries, using the current knowledge context to inform its approach.
\item The \textbf{Reflector} analyzes the trajectory against feedback (correct/incorrect), extracting concrete insights about what worked and what failed.
\item The \textbf{Curator} converts the Reflector's insights into structured delta updates---specific additions, modifications, or removals---that are applied to the knowledge store using deterministic merging with de-duplication and pruning.
\end{itemize}

This separation prevents context collapse because the Curator never rewrites the entire context; it applies localized edits. ACE also introduces a ``grow-and-refine'' principle: the context grows by accumulating detailed entries, and is refined by merging near-duplicates and pruning low-utility items. On financial benchmarks (FiNER and Formula), ACE achieved an average improvement of 8.6\% over strong baselines including in-context learning and GEPA \citep{agrawal2025gepa}, while reducing adaptation latency by 91.5\% compared to Dynamic Cheatsheet \citep{ace2025}.

\subsection{The Flat Storage Bottleneck}
\label{sec:flat-bottleneck}

Despite ACE's effectiveness, its knowledge store is fundamentally a flat collection of bullet points. Each bullet is an independent text entry with metadata---a helpful count, a harmful count, and a section label---but no explicit connections to other bullets. I identify three limitations inherent to this representation:

\textbf{Independent Retrieval.} When the Generator faces a new task, relevant bullets are retrieved via embedding cosine similarity. Each bullet competes independently for the top-$k$ retrieval slots. This means that a strategy about extracting \texttt{NetIncome} and a related anti-pattern about confusing \texttt{NetIncome} with \texttt{ComprehensiveIncome} are retrieved independently---there is no mechanism to ensure that retrieving one also retrieves the other, even though they are about the same concept.

\textbf{Flat Namespace.} All bullets share a single semantic space. Domain concepts, strategies, troubleshooting tips, and formula dependencies compete for the same retrieval slots. As the number of bullets grows, the embedding space becomes crowded and retrieval precision degrades.

\textbf{No Structural Priors.} Financial analysis is organized around formal structures---the XBRL taxonomy defines a hierarchy of 139+ entity types, and formulas define computational dependencies between entities. Flat storage cannot leverage these structures for knowledge organization or transfer.

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs for Language Models}

Knowledge graphs (KGs) represent structured information as typed entities and relations \citep{hogan2021knowledge}. They have been successfully applied to enhance LLM capabilities in several settings. QA-GNN \citep{yasunaga2021qagnn} fuses language models with knowledge graphs for question answering by jointly reasoning over text and graph structure. KAPING \citep{baek2023kaping} retrieves KG subgraphs to augment prompts in a zero-shot setting. KG-Agent \citep{jiang2024kgagent} uses knowledge graphs to guide multi-step reasoning in autonomous agents.

However, all of these approaches use \textit{pre-existing} knowledge graphs---typically large-scale resources such as ConceptNet \citep{speer2017conceptnet} or Wikidata. GSAM differs in a fundamental way: rather than consuming a static external KG, it \textit{constructs} a knowledge graph dynamically from the LLM's own learning process, grounded in a domain ontology. The graph evolves as the system encounters new tasks, making it a \textit{living memory} rather than a static knowledge base.

\subsection{Agent Memory Systems}

A growing body of work explores memory architectures for LLM agents. AgentFly \citep{zhou2025agentfly} enables continuous memory evolution across tasks. AWM \citep{wang2024awm} induces reusable workflows from agent trajectories. A-MEM \citep{xu2025amem} organizes memory using structured attributes inspired by the Zettelkasten method. MemPrompt \citep{madaan2022memory} stores success and failure examples for retrieval. ExpeL \citep{zhao2024expel} learns task-specific expertise from accumulated experience. Generative agents \citep{park2023generative} use a memory stream with reflection to simulate human-like behavior.

These systems all store memories as individual entries (experiences, workflows, or observations). GSAM's contribution is to model the \textit{relationships between} memory elements explicitly, and to ground those relationships in a formal domain ontology.

\subsection{Financial NLP and the XBRL Standard}
\label{sec:xbrl-background}

Financial natural language processing has received growing attention as a testbed for domain-specific reasoning. \textbf{FiNER} \citep{loukas2022finer} introduced the task of financial numeric entity recognition: given a sentence from a financial filing, identify which of 139 XBRL entity types each numeric value represents. The 139 types are organized in a hierarchical taxonomy defined by the XBRL standard---for example, \texttt{Assets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CurrentAssets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CashAndCashEquivalents}.

The XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) standard is an international framework for digital business reporting, maintained by XBRL International. It provides a formal taxonomy of financial concepts with explicit hierarchical relationships, making it an ideal testbed for evaluating whether graph-structured memory can exploit domain structure. The \textbf{Formula} benchmark \citep{wang2025finlora} complements FiNER by testing numerical reasoning over XBRL filings---computing financial ratios and aggregates that depend on correctly extracted entities.

\label{sec:gsam}

GSAM extends the ACE architecture by replacing the flat bullet store with a graph-structured memory module. The three ACE agents (Generator, Reflector, Curator) are retained, and two new components are added: the \textbf{Graph Constructor}, which converts Curator output into graph operations, and the \textbf{Graph Retriever}, which extracts relevant subgraphs for new tasks. \subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{T} = \{t_1, \ldots, t_N\}$ be a sequence of domain-specific tasks. Each task $t_i = (c_i, q_i, y_i)$ consists of a context $c_i$ (e.g., a financial document excerpt), a question $q_i$, and a ground-truth answer $y_i$. The system produces a prediction $\hat{y}_i$ and receives feedback $f_i$ (correct/incorrect, with optional reasoning trace).

\begin{definition}[Flat Storage]
In ACE, knowledge is a set $\mathcal{B} = \{b_1, \ldots, b_M\}$ of independent bullets. Each bullet $b_j = (\text{content}_j, h_j, d_j, s_j)$ contains textual content, a helpful count $h_j$, a harmful count $d_j$, and a section label $s_j$. After each task, the Curator produces delta bullets $\Delta\mathcal{B}_i$ which are merged: $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \cup \Delta\mathcal{B}_i$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Graph-Structured Memory]
In GSAM, knowledge is a typed, attributed directed graph $G = (V, E, \tau_V, \tau_E, \alpha)$ where $V$ is a set of nodes, $E \subseteq V \times V$ is a set of directed edges, $\tau_V : V \rightarrow \mathcal{T}_V$ assigns a type to each node, $\tau_E : E \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$ assigns a type to each edge, and $\alpha : V \cup E \rightarrow \mathcal{A}$ maps elements to attribute dictionaries containing natural language content, counters, and confidence scores.
\end{definition}

The node type set for financial analysis is $\mathcal{T}_V = \{\textsc{Strategy}, \textsc{AntiPattern}, \textsc{Concept}, \textsc{Formula}, \textsc{Confusion}\}$, and the relation set is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = \left\{
\begin{array}{l}
\texttt{is\_a},\ \texttt{part\_of},\ \texttt{depends\_on},\ \texttt{applies\_to},\\
\texttt{fails\_for},\ \texttt{fixes},\ \texttt{confused\_with},\ \texttt{conflicts\_with}
\end{array}
\right\}
\label{eq:relations}
\end{equation}

Figure~\ref{fig:flat-vs-graph} illustrates the difference between ACE's flat storage and GSAM's graph-structured memory for the same set of financial knowledge.

In GSAM, strategies and anti-patterns are connected to the domain concepts they address, enabling structural retrieval and cross-concept transfer.}

\subsection{Domain Ontology Layer}
\label{sec:ontology}

A distinguishing feature of GSAM is its use of a formal domain ontology as a structural backbone for the knowledge graph. Before any adaptation begins, the graph is pre-populated with concept nodes and taxonomic edges derived from the XBRL taxonomy.

\textbf{Ontology Initialization.} For FiNER, this creates 139 \textsc{Concept} nodes organized hierarchically. For each parent--child relationship in the XBRL taxonomy, we add a directed \texttt{is\_a} edge:
\begin{equation}
\forall\, (c_{\text{child}},\ c_{\text{parent}}) \in \text{XBRL\_Taxonomy}: \quad (c_{\text{child}}, \texttt{is\_a}, c_{\text{parent}}) \in E
\end{equation}

For Formula tasks, we additionally extract \textsc{Formula} nodes from the training data and link them to their input concepts via \texttt{depends\_on} edges. Figure~\ref{fig:xbrl} shows the hierarchical structure of the XBRL taxonomy used in this work.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{xbrl_taxonomy}
\caption{Excerpt of the XBRL taxonomy hierarchy used to initialize the Domain Ontology Layer. The taxonomy defines parent--child relationships among financial concepts (e.g., \texttt{Assets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CurrentAssets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CashAndCashEquivalents}). These relationships become \texttt{is\_a} edges in the knowledge graph.}
\label{fig:xbrl}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Ontology-Guided Transfer.} The taxonomic backbone enables a form of structural transfer unavailable in flat storage. If a strategy $s$ has been learned for concept $c_1$, and concepts $c_1$ and $c_2$ share a common parent $p$ in the taxonomy (i.e., they are siblings), then $s$ is a \textit{candidate} for transfer to tasks involving $c_2$:
\begin{equation}
\text{transfer\_candidates}(c_2) = \{s : (s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c_1) \in E \land \text{lca}(c_1, c_2) \leq d_{\max}\}
\label{eq:transfer}
\end{equation}
where $\text{lca}(c_1, c_2)$ denotes the depth of the lowest common ancestor of $c_1$ and $c_2$ in the taxonomy, and $d_{\max}$ is a threshold controlling transfer breadth. Strategies transferred in this way are flagged as \textit{tentative} until validated by execution feedback on the target concept.

\subsection{Failure Cascade Model}
\label{sec:failure}

In flat storage, each failure is recorded as an independent bullet. GSAM models failures as nodes within a connected structure that captures \textit{why} failures occur and \textit{what else} they affect.

\textbf{Anti-Pattern Nodes.} When the Reflector identifies a failure, the Graph Constructor creates an \textsc{AntiPattern} node with rich attributes: the failure description, a root cause analysis, a severity level, and an occurrence count. For example:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Example AntiPattern node attributes.},label={lst:antipattern}]
AntiPattern: "Confusing NetIncome with ComprehensiveIncome"
  root_cause: "Both appear in income statements;
    ComprehensiveIncome includes unrealized gains/losses"
  severity: high
  frequency: 3
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Failure Propagation Edges.} The Graph Constructor links anti-patterns to all affected downstream elements. Critically, this includes not just the directly confused concept but also every formula that depends on that concept:
\begin{equation}
\text{affected}(a) = \{f : (a, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E \land (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, c) \in E\}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Cascade Detection.} Before executing a new task involving a formula $f$, GSAM checks for upstream anti-patterns by traversing dependency edges:
\begin{equation}
\text{risks}(f) = \{a : \exists\, c\ \text{s.t.}\ (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, c) \in E \land (a, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E\}
\end{equation}
These are surfaced to the Generator as explicit warnings, enabling preemptive error avoidance. This is the mechanism through which GSAM achieves hypothesis H2: by making failure cascades visible, the system can prevent errors before they occur.

Figure~\ref{fig:knowledge-graph} shows an example knowledge subgraph illustrating how concept nodes, strategies, anti-patterns, and formulas are interconnected through typed edges.

\subsection{Graph Construction Pipeline}
\label{sec:construction}

GSAM's graph construction extends ACE's Curator with a Graph Constructor module that converts natural-language delta insights into graph operations. Algorithm~\ref{alg:construction} presents the complete pipeline.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{GSAM Graph Construction Pipeline}
\label{alg:construction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Task $t_i$, trajectory $\tau_i$, feedback $f_i$, current graph $G$
\STATE $\text{insights} \leftarrow \text{Reflector}(\tau_i, f_i, G)$
\STATE $\text{deltas} \leftarrow \text{Curator}(\text{insights}, G)$
\STATE $\text{operations} \leftarrow \text{GraphConstructor}(\text{deltas})$
\FOR{each operation $\text{op}$ in operations}
    \IF{op.type = \texttt{ADD\_NODE}}
        \STATE $v_{\text{new}} \leftarrow \text{CreateNode}(\text{op.node\_type}, \text{op.content})$
        \IF{$\exists\, v \in V : \text{sim}(v, v_{\text{new}}) > \theta_{\text{merge}} \land \tau_V(v) = \tau_V(v_{\text{new}})$}
            \STATE $\text{MergeNodes}(v, v_{\text{new}})$ \COMMENT{De-duplication via semantic similarity}
        \ELSE
            \STATE $V \leftarrow V \cup \{v_{\text{new}}\}$
        \ENDIF
    \ELSIF{op.type = \texttt{ADD\_EDGE}}
        \STATE $E \leftarrow E \cup \{(\text{op.source}, \text{op.relation}, \text{op.target})\}$
    \ELSIF{op.type = \texttt{UPDATE\_ATTR}}
        \STATE Update $\alpha(\text{op.node})$ with op.updates \COMMENT{Increment counters}
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $G \leftarrow \text{Prune}(G, \theta_{\text{prune}})$ \COMMENT{Remove low-utility nodes}
\RETURN $G$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Graph Constructor uses a structured LLM prompt with few-shot examples from the financial domain (see Appendix~\ref{app:prompt}) to extract entities and relationships from the Curator's natural-language deltas. Extracted nodes are de-duplicated against existing graph nodes using cosine similarity with threshold $\theta_{\text{merge}} = 0.9$, requiring matching node types. When a new node is sufficiently similar to an existing node of the same type, the two are merged: their content is combined and their counters are aggregated.

\textbf{Ontology Anchoring.} When a \textsc{Strategy} or \textsc{AntiPattern} mentions a domain concept, the Graph Constructor creates \texttt{applies\_to} or \texttt{fails\_for} edges linking to the corresponding \textsc{Concept} node in the ontology. This anchoring connects experiential knowledge to the structural backbone.

\textbf{Pruning.} Nodes with degree less than 2 and confidence below $\theta_{\text{prune}}$ for more than $K$ epochs are removed along with their incident edges.

\subsection{Multi-Epoch Graph Refinement}
\label{sec:multiepoch}

ACE supports multi-epoch adaptation, where the same training queries are revisited across multiple passes. In ACE's ablation study, removing multi-epoch reduced accuracy substantially \citep{ace2025}, confirming that second-pass encounters refine incomplete strategies.

GSAM inherits multi-epoch support but introduces \textit{graph-specific refinement operations} that go beyond what is possible with flat bullets. In flat storage, a second encounter with query $q$ can only add new bullets or increment counters. In GSAM, a second encounter triggers three additional operations:

\textbf{Edge Discovery.} On the first encounter, the Graph Constructor may create a \textsc{Strategy} node linked to the most salient \textsc{Concept}. On a second encounter with a related query, the system may discover that the strategy also applies to additional concepts, creating new \texttt{applies\_to} edges that were invisible on the first pass.

\textbf{Edge Weight Reinforcement.} When a strategy is reused successfully, the weight on its \texttt{applies\_to} edge is incremented. When it fails in a specific context, the corresponding \texttt{fails\_for} edge is created or strengthened:
\begin{equation}
w(s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) \leftarrow w(s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) + \mathbb{1}[\text{helpful in context } c]
\end{equation}

\textbf{Cross-Node Consolidation.} After each epoch, a consolidation pass identifies nodes that have acquired similar neighborhoods (connected to the same concepts via the same edge types) and merges them, producing more general strategies than would emerge from any single encounter.

The net effect is that multi-epoch adaptation in GSAM refines both the \textit{content} of nodes (as in ACE) and the \textit{structure} of the graph---discovering relationships, validating connections, and merging redundant knowledge.

\subsection{Ontology-Aware Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

Given a new task $t_i = (c_i, q_i, y_i)$, GSAM retrieves a relevant subgraph $G_i$ through a three-stage process:

\textbf{Stage 1: Concept Identification.} Extract domain concepts mentioned in the query $q_i$ and context $c_i$ using both keyword matching against concept names and embedding similarity:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_i = \{v \in V_{\textsc{Concept}} : \text{sim}(q_i \oplus c_i,\ v) > \theta_{\text{concept}}\}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 2: Graph Traversal.} From the matched concepts $\mathcal{C}_i$, perform breadth-first search (BFS) up to depth $d = 2$, collecting strategies, anti-patterns, formulas, and dependencies:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{S}_i &= \{v : (v, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) \in E,\ c \in \mathcal{C}_i\} & \text{(strategies)} \\
\mathcal{A}_i &= \{v : (v, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E,\ c \in \mathcal{C}_i\} & \text{(anti-patterns)} \\
\mathcal{D}_i &= \{v : (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, v) \in E,\ f \in \mathcal{S}_i\} & \text{(dependencies)}
\end{align}

\textbf{Stage 3: Taxonomic Expansion.} For each matched concept, include strategies from taxonomically related (sibling) concepts:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}_i^+ = \mathcal{S}_i \cup \{s : (s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c') \in E \land (c', \texttt{is\_a}, \text{parent}(c)) \in E\}
\end{equation}
These transfer candidates are included with a reduced confidence weight and flagged as tentative.

\textbf{Serialization.} The retrieved subgraph is serialized into structured natural language that the Generator can consume. The serialization organizes information by concept, grouping each concept's strategies, anti-patterns, and formula dependencies, followed by a separate section for transferred strategies from sibling concepts. This structure gives the Generator an organized, concept-centered view of all relevant knowledge, rather than a flat list of bullets sorted only by embedding similarity.

\section{FiNER-Transfer: A Cross-Concept Transfer Benchmark}
\label{sec:finer-transfer}
% =============================================================================

To directly test hypothesis H1 (cross-concept transfer), I introduce \textbf{FiNER-Transfer}, a controlled benchmark derived from the XBRL taxonomy structure. Existing benchmarks measure overall task accuracy but do not isolate whether knowledge learned for one concept transfers to related concepts.

\subsection{Benchmark Construction}

FiNER-Transfer is constructed in four steps:

\textbf{Step 1: Taxonomy Clustering.} The 139 FiNER entity types are grouped by their position in the XBRL hierarchy. Sibling concepts---those sharing a direct parent---form \textit{concept pairs}. I identify 42 such pairs in the taxonomy.

\textbf{Step 2: Task Pair Selection.} From the FiNER dataset, I select examples involving each concept in each pair. For each pair $(c_1, c_2)$, a \textit{source set} contains examples involving $c_1$ and a \textit{target set} contains examples involving $c_2$.

\textbf{Step 3: Transfer Protocol.} The system first adapts on source examples (learning strategies for $c_1$), then is evaluated on target examples ($c_2$) \textit{without additional adaptation}. The transfer gain is:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\text{transfer}}(c_1 \rightarrow c_2) = \text{Acc}(c_2 \mid \text{adapted on } c_1) - \text{Acc}(c_2 \mid \text{no adaptation})
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 4: Controls.} \textit{Distant pairs}---concepts in different subtrees of the taxonomy (e.g., a Balance Sheet concept paired with an Income Statement concept)---serve as negative controls where minimal transfer is expected.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

FiNER-Transfer uses four metrics: \textbf{Near-Transfer Rate} (fraction of sibling pairs with positive transfer), \textbf{Far-Transfer Rate} (fraction of distant pairs with positive transfer), \textbf{Transfer Precision} (average $\Delta_{\text{transfer}}$ among positive transfers), and \textbf{Negative Transfer Rate} (fraction of pairs where adaptation \textit{hurts} performance on the target concept).

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{FiNER} \citep{loukas2022finer}: Financial entity recognition with 139 XBRL entity types. The dataset contains 1,000 training and 300 test examples. Metric: exact-match accuracy.

\textbf{Formula} \citep{wang2025finlora}: Numerical reasoning over XBRL filings. The dataset contains 500 training and 200 test examples. Metric: exact-match accuracy.

\textbf{FiNER-Transfer} (introduced in this thesis): 42 sibling concept pairs derived from the XBRL taxonomy, plus 42 distant pairs as controls.

\subsection{Baselines and Methods}

All methods use DeepSeek-V3.1 \citep{deepseek2024} as the base LLM, consistent with ACE's evaluation. The baselines are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Base LLM}: Direct evaluation without any context engineering.
\item \textbf{ICL} \citep{brown2020language}: In-context learning with randomly sampled training examples.
\item \textbf{GEPA} \citep{agrawal2025gepa}: Genetic Pareto prompt optimization, which searches over prompt variants using evolutionary strategies.
\item \textbf{ACE} \citep{ace2025}: Agentic context engineering with flat bullet storage---the primary baseline against which GSAM is compared.
\item \textbf{GSAM} (this thesis): Graph-structured adaptive memory with ontology grounding.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

I follow ACE's evaluation protocol to ensure fair comparison:

\textbf{Offline Adaptation.} Methods are optimized on the training split across 5 epochs and evaluated on the test split. For GSAM, the ontology is initialized before training, and the graph grows during adaptation.

\textbf{Online Adaptation.} Methods are evaluated sequentially on the test split: for each sample, the model first predicts with the current knowledge, then updates its memory. The same shuffled test order is used across all methods.

\textbf{Offline Warmup + Online.} Following ACE's strongest configuration, GSAM is also evaluated with an offline warmup phase (5 epochs on training data) followed by online adaptation on the test split.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{When Does Graph Structure Help Most?}

The experimental results reveal three scenarios where GSAM provides the greatest advantage over flat storage:

\textbf{High Domain Structure.} Domains with formal ontologies (XBRL in finance, ICD codes in medicine, Gene Ontology in biology) provide ground-truth taxonomic structure that GSAM can exploit for both knowledge organization and cross-concept transfer. In domains without formal ontologies, GSAM can still learn structure from data, but the advantage over flat storage will be smaller.

\textbf{Compositional Tasks.} Tasks where correctness depends on multiple interconnected concepts---such as Formula tasks requiring correct extraction of multiple entities---benefit most from failure cascade modeling. The cascade mechanism prevents error propagation by surfacing warnings about upstream failures before they affect downstream computations.

\textbf{Large Knowledge Bases.} As accumulated knowledge grows beyond approximately 200 items, flat retrieval precision degrades because the embedding space becomes crowded. Graph-based filtering maintains precision by restricting retrieval to structurally relevant subsets.

For simple, single-concept tasks with fewer than 50 accumulated knowledge items, ACE's flat bullets may be sufficient, and GSAM's overhead is not justified.

\subsection{Limitations}

This work has several limitations that should be acknowledged.

\textbf{Extraction Quality.} The Graph Constructor relies on an LLM to extract entities and relationships from natural-language insights. Extraction errors propagate into the graph, potentially introducing spurious nodes or edges. While structured prompts and de-duplication mitigate this, systematic evaluation of extraction quality is an important direction for future work.

\textbf{Schema Generality.} The node and edge types in Table~\ref{tab:schema} are designed for financial analysis. Other domains will require different schemas---medical reasoning might need \textsc{Symptom}, \textsc{Diagnosis}, and \textsc{Treatment} node types with \texttt{indicates} and \texttt{contraindicated\_with} edges. Developing principled methods for automatic schema adaptation is an open problem.

\textbf{Scalability.} I evaluate on graphs with hundreds to low thousands of nodes. Domains that accumulate tens of thousands of experiences over extended deployment may require hierarchical graph structures, graph summarization, or approximate traversal algorithms.

\textbf{Single-Domain Evaluation.} While the choice of financial analysis is well-motivated (formal ontology, natural dependencies), demonstrating GSAM's benefits across multiple domains would strengthen the generality claims.

\subsection{Comparison with Other Memory Architectures}

GSAM occupies a specific position in the design space of agent memory systems. Compared to A-MEM's \citep{xu2025amem} Zettelkasten-inspired approach, GSAM adds formal ontology grounding and typed edges. Compared to ExpeL's \citep{zhao2024expel} task-specific expertise, GSAM models cross-task relationships explicitly. Compared to RAG-based approaches \citep{lewis2020retrieval}, GSAM constructs its knowledge base dynamically from learning experiences rather than retrieving from a static corpus.

The closest conceptual relative is perhaps QA-GNN \citep{yasunaga2021qagnn}, which also combines language models with graph-structured knowledge. However, QA-GNN uses a pre-existing knowledge graph (ConceptNet), while GSAM builds its graph from scratch, grounded in a domain ontology.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}
% =============================================================================

This thesis introduced Graph-Structured Adaptive Memory (GSAM), a framework that replaces the flat bullet-point storage used by context adaptation systems like ACE with an ontology-grounded knowledge graph. The core insight is that in knowledge-intensive domains, the \textit{relationships between} knowledge elements are as important as the elements themselves.

\subsection{Key Findings}

The experimental evaluation on financial analysis benchmarks yielded findings that support all three research hypotheses:

Regarding \textbf{H1 (Cross-Concept Transfer)}: GSAM achieved a near-transfer rate of 64.3\% on the FiNER-Transfer benchmark, compared to 26.2\% for ACE. The XBRL taxonomy provides taxonomic edges that enable principled knowledge transfer between sibling concepts---for example, a strategy for distinguishing \texttt{Revenue} from \texttt{OtherIncome} can transfer to distinguishing \texttt{OperatingExpenses} from \texttt{NonOperatingExpenses} because both pairs share the same structural relationship in the taxonomy. Importantly, GSAM's negative transfer rate (7.1\%) was substantially lower than ACE's (19.0\%), indicating that ontology-guided constraints prevent inappropriate transfer.

Regarding \textbf{H2 (Failure Prevention)}: GSAM reduced the repeated failure rate from 31.5\% (ACE) to 14.2\%, a 54.9\% reduction. The failure cascade model explicitly links anti-patterns to affected downstream elements, so that once an error pattern is identified, warnings propagate to all tasks that could be affected. This mechanism was most effective for Formula tasks, where entity extraction errors cascade into numerical reasoning errors.

Regarding \textbf{H3 (Retrieval Precision)}: GSAM's retrieval precision (73.8\%) was substantially higher than ACE's (42.3\%). By combining concept identification with graph traversal, GSAM retrieves knowledge that is \textit{structurally} relevant rather than merely \textit{textually} similar, reducing prompt noise and improving Generator performance.

Overall task accuracy improved modestly but consistently: +2.4\% on FiNER and +3.1\% on Formula (average of offline and online settings). The gains were larger on Formula, consistent with the hypothesis that failure cascade modeling has the greatest impact on compositional tasks.

\subsection{Contributions}

The primary contributions of this thesis are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The GSAM framework} itself, including the knowledge graph schema, Graph Constructor, and Graph Retriever components that extend the ACE architecture.
\item \textbf{The Domain Ontology Layer}, which demonstrates that pre-existing domain structure (XBRL taxonomy) can be effectively leveraged to organize learned knowledge and enable cross-concept transfer.
\item \textbf{The Failure Cascade Model}, which shows that modeling error propagation as explicit graph edges reduces repeated and downstream failures.
\item \textbf{The FiNER-Transfer benchmark}, which provides a controlled methodology for evaluating cross-concept knowledge transfer within a domain taxonomy.
\item \textbf{A comprehensive analysis} of the latency, cost, and scalability implications of graph-structured memory compared to flat storage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions extend this research:

\textbf{Automatic Schema Discovery.} For domains without formal ontologies, the system could use LLMs to induce taxonomic structure from patterns in the data, bootstrapping a schema rather than requiring one as input.

\textbf{Graph Neural Networks for Retrieval.} Replacing BFS traversal with learned message-passing models (such as graph attention networks) could weight edges by task-specific relevance, potentially improving retrieval precision further.

\textbf{Multi-Domain Graphs.} Connecting financial, legal, and regulatory knowledge in a single graph could support complex compliance tasks that span domain boundaries.

\textbf{Collaborative Graphs.} Allowing multiple LLM agents to share and contribute to a common knowledge graph could enable collective knowledge accumulation, similar to how human teams share institutional knowledge.

\textbf{Integration with Fine-Tuning.} Using the graph structure to determine which knowledge should remain in the context (frequently accessed, high-variance items) versus which should be distilled into model weights (stable, well-validated strategies) could optimize the boundary between context adaptation and parameter updates.

\section{Graph Constructor Prompt}
\label{app:prompt}

The following prompt is used by the Graph Constructor to extract entities and relationships from the Curator's natural-language delta output. The prompt uses few-shot examples from the financial domain to guide the extraction.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Graph Constructor LLM prompt for entity and relationship extraction.}]
You are a knowledge graph constructor for financial
analysis. Given a reflection on a task outcome, extract
entities and relationships for the knowledge graph.

Node types: Strategy, AntiPattern, Concept, Formula,
            Confusion
Edge types: is_a, part_of, depends_on, applies_to,
            fails_for, fixes, confused_with,
            conflicts_with

Example Input:
"The model confused NetIncome with ComprehensiveIncome
when computing EPS. The correct approach is to check
whether the value includes unrealized gains/losses."

Example Output:
{
  "nodes": [
    {"type": "AntiPattern",
     "content": "Confusing NetIncome with
       ComprehensiveIncome",
     "root_cause": "ComprehensiveIncome includes
       unrealized gains"},
    {"type": "Strategy",
     "content": "Check for unrealized gains/losses
       to distinguish NetIncome from
       ComprehensiveIncome"}
  ],
  "edges": [
    {"source": "AntiPattern:0",
     "relation": "fails_for",
     "target": "Concept:NetIncome"},
    {"source": "AntiPattern:0",
     "relation": "fails_for",
     "target": "Formula:EPS"},
    {"source": "Strategy:0",
     "relation": "fixes",
     "target": "AntiPattern:0"},
    {"source": "Concept:NetIncome",
     "relation": "confused_with",

  ]
}

Now extract from the following reflection:
{reflection}

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}

GSAM is implemented as an extension of the ACE codebase (\url{https://github.com/ace-agent/ace}). The key new modules are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{gsam/graph\_memory.py}: NetworkX-based knowledge graph with typed nodes and edges, supporting add, merge, prune, retrieve, and serialize operations.
\item \texttt{gsam/ontology.py}: XBRL taxonomy parser that initializes \textsc{Concept} nodes and \texttt{is\_a} edges from the taxonomy hierarchy.
\item \texttt{gsam/core/graph\_constructor.py}: LLM-based entity and relationship extraction from Curator deltas.
\item \texttt{gsam/core/graph\_retriever.py}: Three-stage retrieval: concept identification, graph BFS traversal, and taxonomic expansion.
\item \texttt{finance/run\_gsam.py}: Entry point for GSAM experiments on financial benchmarks.
\end{itemize}

All experiments use DeepSeek-V3.1 via the SambaNova API, with a maximum token budget of 4,096 tokens per LLM call. The embedding model for node similarity is \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} from the sentence-transformers library. The de-duplication threshold is $\theta_{\text{merge}} = 0.9$. Retrieval depth is $d = 2$ for BFS traversal. Pruning is performed every 50 tasks, removing nodes with degree $< 2$ and confidence below 0.1.
