\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) are now essential tools in almost every area of computing, which includes software engineering, scientific research, financial analysis, and legal reasoning \citep{brown2020language, openai2023gpt4}. Even though LLMs are very good at a lot of things, they have difficulties with specific domains and areas that require accurate, and detailed knowledge. These areas have knowledge that is constantly changing and needs to be up-to-date. Large language models are not as good, in these domains. They struggle with structured information. This constitutes a constraint of language models. A model trained on broad internet data does not inherently understand the hierarchical relationships within the XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) taxonomy used in financial reporting, nor can it reason about how an error in identifying one financial entity propagates through downstream formulas \citep{loukas2022finer}.

Two broad strategies have emerged to address this gap. \textit{Fine-tuning} updates the model's weights on domain-specific data \citep{wang2025finlora}, but is expensive, non-interpretable, and difficult to update incrementally. \textit{Context adaptation} (also called context engineering) instead modifies the \textit{inputs} to the model---injecting instructions, strategies, examples, or evidence into the prompt without altering weights \citep{ace2025}. Context adaptation is interpretable (the injected knowledge is readable text), cheap to update (no retraining), and shareable across models.

Recent context adaptation frameworks have shown impressive results. Dynamic Cheatsheet \citep{suzgun2025dynamic} introduced the idea of an evolving memory of strategies accumulated during test-time inference. ACE (Agentic Context Engineering) \citep{ace2025} addressed two critical failure modes of earlier approaches---\textit{brevity bias} (where optimization collapses toward overly concise summaries that lose domain detail) and \textit{context collapse} (where iterative LLM-driven rewriting gradually degrades accumulated knowledge)---through a multi-agent architecture with incremental delta updates. ACE achieved average gains of 8.6\% on financial benchmarks and 10.6\% on agent tasks, while reducing adaptation latency by 86.9\% compared to prior methods \citep{ace2025}.

However, both Dynamic Cheatsheet and ACE share a fundamental architectural limitation: they store accumulated knowledge as \textit{flat collections of bullet points}. Each bullet is an independent unit with metadata (helpful and harmful counters), but with no explicit connections to other bullets. This design discards a signal that is critically important in specialized domains: the \textit{relationships between knowledge elements}.

\subsection{Research Objectives}

In this thesis, I introduce \textbf{GSAM (Graph-Structured Adaptive Memory)}, a framework that replaces flat bullet-point storage with an ontology-grounded knowledge graph. In GSAM, domain concepts, strategies, anti-patterns, formulas, and their interrelationships are modeled as typed nodes and edges within a directed graph. The framework extends ACE's Generator--Reflector--Curator architecture with two new components: a \textbf{Graph Constructor} that builds and maintains the knowledge graph from learning experiences, and a \textbf{Graph Retriever} that extracts relevant subgraphs for new tasks through a combination of embedding similarity and structural graph traversal.

The research targets three hypotheses:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=1.5cm]
\item[\textbf{H1:}] Graph-structured memory with ontology grounding improves cross-concept knowledge transfer compared to flat storage.
\item[\textbf{H2:}] Explicit failure cascade modeling reduces repeated and downstream errors.
\item[\textbf{H3:}] Ontology-aware retrieval achieves higher precision than embedding-only retrieval.
\end{description}

I evaluate GSAM on two financial analysis benchmarks---FiNER \citep{loukas2022finer} (139-type entity recognition) and Formula \citep{wang2025finlora} (numerical reasoning over financial filings)---and additionally introduce \textbf{FiNER-Transfer}, a new benchmark specifically designed to measure cross-concept transfer within the XBRL taxonomy. The choice to focus exclusively on domain-specific financial tasks, rather than general agent benchmarks such as AppWorld \citep{trivedi2024appworld}, is deliberate: financial analysis provides formal ontological structure (the XBRL taxonomy), natural dependency graphs (formulas depend on entity extraction), and compositional error cascades---all of which are naturally captured by graphs but invisible in flat storage.

\subsection{Thesis Structure}

The rest of this thesis is set up like this: \textbf{Section 2} looks at whats been written about adapting to different situations, knowledge graphs and using computers to understand finance. \textbf{Section 3} explains our GSAM framework, including how we set up our knowledge graph, the layer that helps make sense of it all, how we model, what happens when things go wrong, and how we find the information we need. \textbf{Section 4} talks about how we put the FiNER-Transfer benchmark. \textbf{Section 5} goes into detail about how we designed our experiment and what we found out. \textbf{Section 6} takes a look at how using graph-structured memory affects how long things take, and how much it costs. \textbf{Section 7} discusses what we learned, what didn't work well and what we plan to do next. \textbf{Section 8} sums up what we accomplished.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Large Language Models and Context Windows}

The transformer architecture, introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}, fundamentally changed natural language processing by processing entire input sequences simultaneously through self-attention mechanisms rather than sequentially. Scaling this architecture to billions of parameters produced models such as GPT-3 \citep{brown2020language}, GPT-4 \citep{openai2023gpt4}, LLaMA \citep{touvron2023llama}, and DeepSeek \citep{deepseek2024}. These models excel in learning from limited examples and perform effectively across diverse tasks.

The key to this few-shot learning capability is that a model's behavior can change substantially based on what appears in the \textit{context window}---the input provided alongside the query \citep{brown2020language}. This insight motivated a shift away from fine-tuning (modifying model weights) toward \textit{context engineering} (modifying inputs) as a more flexible approach to domain adaptation.

Several methods exemplify this context-centric paradigm. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) \citep{lewis2020retrieval} retrieves relevant documents to augment the context. Chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain} elicits step-by-step reasoning by structuring the context appropriately. Decomposed prompting \citep{khot2022decomposed} breaks complex tasks into smaller, more manageable sub-tasks. These methods demonstrate that \textit{what} is in the context matters as much as \textit{how} the model was trained. The transformer architecture enables few-shot learning, and few-shot learning is a fundamental benefit of the transformer architecture.

\subsection{From Dynamic Cheatsheet to ACE}

The paradigm of how we enable machines to understand and adapt is shifting from static prompts to systems that can remember and learn over time. \textbf{Dynamic Cheatsheet} \citep{suzgun2025dynamic} introduced the concept of a ``cheatsheet''---an external memory that a model could use to remember strategies that work. As the model performed tasks, it would reflect on its actions and add successful approaches to the cheatsheet. This was a promising idea that improved performance, but it suffered from two significant problems.

First, the model exhibited \textit{brevity bias}: it would attempt to compress the cheatsheet shorter and shorter, causing it to lose important details over time.

Second, when the model kept rewriting the cheatsheet, the information would degrade progressively---a phenomenon known as \textit{context collapse}.

\textbf{ACE (Agentic Context Engineering)} \citep{ace2025} was developed to address these problems through a three-component architecture that separates responsibilities:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item The \textbf{Generator} produces plans for new queries, using existing knowledge to inform its approach.
\item The \textbf{Reflector} analyzes how the plan worked and determines what was successful and what failed.
\item The \textbf{Curator} takes what the Reflector learned and adds it to the cheatsheet in a structured way, preventing the information from becoming disorganized.
\end{itemize}

This architecture prevents context collapse because the Curator only makes localized changes to the cheatsheet rather than rewriting it entirely. ACE also implements a ``grow-and-refine'' principle: the context grows by accumulating detailed entries and is refined by merging near-duplicates and pruning items that are not helpful. When evaluated on benchmarks, ACE significantly outperformed other approaches including in-context learning and GEPA \citep{agrawal2025gepa}. ACE was also substantially faster than Dynamic Cheatsheet, achieving adaptation 91.5\% faster \citep{ace2025}.

\subsection{The Flat Storage Bottleneck}
\label{sec:flat-bottleneck}

ACE works well, but its knowledge storage is basically just a flat list of bullet points. Each bullet is an independent text entry with metadata---a helpful count, a harmful count, and a section label---but no explicit connections to other bullets. I identify three limitations inherent to this representation:

\textbf{Independent Retrieval.} When the Generator faces a new task, relevant bullets are retrieved via embedding cosine similarity. Each bullet competes independently for the top-$k$ retrieval slots. This means that a strategy about extracting \texttt{NetIncome} and a related anti-pattern about confusing \texttt{NetIncome} with \texttt{ComprehensiveIncome} are retrieved independently---there is no mechanism to ensure that retrieving one also retrieves the other, even though they are about the same concept.

\textbf{Flat Namespace.} All bullets share a single semantic space. Domain concepts, strategies, troubleshooting tips, and formula dependencies compete for the same retrieval slots. As the number of bullets grows, the embedding space becomes crowded and retrieval precision degrades.

\textbf{No Structural Priors.} Financial analysis is organized around formal structures---the XBRL taxonomy defines a hierarchy of 139+ entity types, and formulas define computational dependencies between entities. Flat storage cannot leverage these structures for knowledge organization or transfer.

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs for Language Models}

Knowledge graphs (KGs) represent structured information as typed entities and relations \citep{hogan2021knowledge}. They have been successfully applied to enhance LLM capabilities in several settings. QA-GNN \citep{yasunaga2021qagnn} fuses language models with knowledge graphs for question answering by jointly reasoning over text and graph structure. KAPING \citep{baek2023kaping} retrieves KG subgraphs to augment prompts in a zero-shot setting. KG-Agent \citep{jiang2024kgagent} uses knowledge graphs to guide multi-step reasoning in autonomous agents.

However, all of these approaches use \textit{pre-existing} knowledge graphs, typically large-scale resources such as ConceptNet \citep{speer2017conceptnet} or Wikidata. What makes GSAM different, and perhaps this is its most interesting quality, is that it does not simply pull from some external knowledge graph that already exists. Instead, it builds one. The graph grows out of what the model actually learns while working through tasks, anchored to a domain ontology that gives the structure meaning. As the system encounters new problems, the graph changes with it. So rather than treating knowledge as something fixed and external, GSAM treats it as something alive, something that develops alongside the learning process itself.

\subsection{Agent Memory Systems}

A growing body of work explores memory architectures for LLM agents. AgentFly \citep{zhou2025agentfly} enables continuous memory evolution across tasks. AWM \citep{wang2024awm} induces reusable workflows from agent trajectories. A-MEM \citep{xu2025amem} organizes memory using structured attributes inspired by the Zettelkasten method. MemPrompt \citep{madaan2022memory} stores success and failure examples for retrieval. ExpeL \citep{zhao2024expel} learns task-specific expertise from accumulated experience. Generative agents \citep{park2023generative} use a memory stream with reflection to simulate human-like behavior.

These systems all store memories as individual entries (experiences, workflows, or observations). GSAM's contribution is to model the \textit{relationships between} memory elements explicitly, and to ground those relationships in a formal domain ontology.

\subsection{Financial NLP and the XBRL Standard}
\label{sec:xbrl-background}

Financial natural language processing has received growing attention as a testbed for domain-specific reasoning. \textbf{FiNER} \citep{loukas2022finer} introduced the task of financial numeric entity recognition: given a sentence from a financial filing, identify which of 139 XBRL entity types each numeric value represents. The 139 types are organized in a hierarchical taxonomy defined by the XBRL standard---for example, \texttt{Assets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CurrentAssets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CashAndCashEquivalents}.

The XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) standard is an international framework for digital business reporting, maintained by XBRL International. It provides a formal taxonomy of financial concepts with explicit hierarchical relationships, making it an ideal testbed for evaluating whether graph-structured memory can exploit domain structure. Then there is the \textbf{Formula} benchmark \citep{wang2025finlora}, which adds another layer to this. Where FiNER asks the model to recognize what something is, Formula asks it to actually do something with that knowledge, to compute ratios and aggregates from XBRL filings. The catch is that these calculations depend on getting the entity extraction right in the first place. So in a sense, Formula tests whether the model can follow through on what it learned, not just whether it can label things correctly.

\section{The GSAM Framework}
\label{sec:gsam}
% =============================================================================

At its core, GSAM builds on the ACE architecture, but with one significant change: it swaps out the flat bullet store for a graph-structured memory module. The three original ACE agents, the Generator, the Reflector, and the Curator, remain intact. What GSAM introduces, though, are two new components. The first is the \textbf{Graph Constructor}, which takes whatever the Curator outputs and translates it into graph operations. The second is the \textbf{Graph Retriever}, responsible for pulling out relevant subgraphs whenever a new task comes in. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows how all of this fits together.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{gsam_framework.png}
\caption{An overview of the GSAM architecture. The Generator, Reflector, and Curator carry over from ACE without modification. What GSAM contributes are two additions: a Graph Constructor that turns Curator deltas into graph operations, and a Graph Retriever that fetches relevant subgraphs for each incoming query. The Knowledge Graph $G$ takes the place of ACE's flat bullet store. As for data flow, a query first enters the Generator alongside retrieved context, then the trajectory moves through the Reflector and Curator, and finally, the resulting deltas get converted into graph operations by the Graph Constructor.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{T} = \{t_1, \ldots, t_N\}$ be a sequence of domain-specific tasks. Each task $t_i = (c_i, q_i, y_i)$ consists of a context $c_i$ (e.g., a financial document excerpt), a question $q_i$, and a ground-truth answer $y_i$. The system produces a prediction $\hat{y}_i$ and receives feedback $f_i$ (correct/incorrect, with optional reasoning trace).

\begin{definition}[Flat Storage]
In ACE, knowledge is a set $\mathcal{B} = \{b_1, \ldots, b_M\}$ of independent bullets. Each bullet $b_j = (\text{content}_j, h_j, d_j, s_j)$ contains textual content, a helpful count $h_j$, a harmful count $d_j$, and a section label $s_j$. After each task, the Curator produces delta bullets $\Delta\mathcal{B}_i$ which are merged: $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \cup \Delta\mathcal{B}_i$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Graph-Structured Memory]
In GSAM, knowledge is a typed, attributed directed graph $G = (V, E, \tau_V, \tau_E, \alpha)$ where $V$ is a set of nodes, $E \subseteq V \times V$ is a set of directed edges, $\tau_V : V \rightarrow \mathcal{T}_V$ assigns a type to each node, $\tau_E : E \rightarrow \mathcal{R}$ assigns a type to each edge, and $\alpha : V \cup E \rightarrow \mathcal{A}$ maps elements to attribute dictionaries containing natural language content, counters, and confidence scores.
\end{definition}

The node type set for financial analysis is $\mathcal{T}_V = \{\textsc{Strategy}, \textsc{AntiPattern}, \textsc{Concept}, \textsc{Formula}, \textsc{Confusion}\}$, and the relation set is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = \left\{
\begin{array}{l}
\texttt{is\_a},\ \texttt{part\_of},\ \texttt{depends\_on},\ \texttt{applies\_to},\\
\texttt{fails\_for},\ \texttt{fixes},\ \texttt{confused\_with},\ \texttt{conflicts\_with}
\end{array}
\right\}
\label{eq:relations}
\end{equation}

Figure~\ref{fig:flat-vs-graph} illustrates the difference between ACE's flat storage and GSAM's graph-structured memory for the same set of financial knowledge.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure2}
\caption{Comparison of ACE's flat bullet storage (left) and GSAM's knowledge graph (right) for the same financial analysis knowledge. In flat storage, each bullet is an independent entry retrieved by embedding similarity. In GSAM, strategies and anti-patterns are connected to the domain concepts they address, enabling structural retrieval and cross-concept transfer.}
\label{fig:flat-vs-graph}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Domain Ontology Layer}
\label{sec:ontology}

A distinguishing feature of GSAM is its use of a formal domain ontology as a structural backbone for the knowledge graph. Before any adaptation begins, the graph is pre-populated with concept nodes and taxonomic edges derived from the XBRL taxonomy.

\textbf{Ontology Initialization.} For FiNER, this creates 139 \textsc{Concept} nodes organized hierarchically. For each parent--child relationship in the XBRL taxonomy, we add a directed \texttt{is\_a} edge:
\begin{equation}
\forall\, (c_{\text{child}},\ c_{\text{parent}}) \in \text{XBRL\_Taxonomy}: \quad (c_{\text{child}}, \texttt{is\_a}, c_{\text{parent}}) \in E
\end{equation}

For Formula tasks, we additionally extract \textsc{Formula} nodes from the training data and link them to their input concepts via \texttt{depends\_on} edges. Figure~\ref{fig:xbrl} shows the hierarchical structure of the XBRL taxonomy used in this work.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figure3}
\caption{Excerpt of the XBRL taxonomy hierarchy used to initialize the Domain Ontology Layer. The taxonomy defines parent--child relationships among financial concepts (e.g., \texttt{Assets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CurrentAssets} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{CashAndCashEquivalents}). These relationships become \texttt{is\_a} edges in the knowledge graph.}
\label{fig:xbrl}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Ontology-Guided Transfer.} Here is where the taxonomic backbone really starts to pay off. With a flat storage system, there is no natural way to say that two concepts are related, that what works for one might work for the other. But with the graph structure, that kind of transfer becomes possible. Say a strategy $s$ has been learned for concept $c_1$. If $c_1$ and $c_2$ happen to share a common parent $p$ in the taxonomy, meaning they are siblings in some sense, then $s$ becomes a reasonable candidate for transfer to tasks involving $c_2$. More formally:
\begin{equation}
\text{transfer\_candidates}(c_2) = \{s : (s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c_1) \in E \land \text{lca}(c_1, c_2) \leq d_{\max}\}
\label{eq:transfer}
\end{equation}
The notation $\text{lca}(c_1, c_2)$ refers to the depth of the lowest common ancestor of $c_1$ and $c_2$ within the taxonomy, and $d_{\max}$ acts as a threshold that controls how broadly we allow transfer to happen. Of course, this does not mean we blindly trust transferred strategies. Any strategy that gets transferred in this way is flagged as \textit{tentative}, and it stays that way until execution feedback on the target concept actually validates it. This cautious approach seems necessary, since structural similarity does not always guarantee functional equivalence.

\subsection{Failure Cascade Model}
\label{sec:failure}

With flat storage, every failure just gets logged as its own separate bullet point, disconnected from everything else. GSAM takes a different approach. It treats failures as nodes within a connected structure, one that captures not only \textit{why} something went wrong but also \textit{what else} might be affected as a result.

\textbf{Anti-Pattern Nodes.} Whenever the Reflector spots a failure, the Graph Constructor responds by creating an \textsc{AntiPattern} node. These nodes are not just simple labels. They carry rich attributes: a description of what failed, an analysis of the root cause, a severity level, and a count of how many times this particular failure has occurred. To give a concrete sense of what this looks like:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Example AntiPattern node attributes.},label={lst:antipattern}]
AntiPattern: "Confusing NetIncome with ComprehensiveIncome"
  root_cause: "Both appear in income statements;
    ComprehensiveIncome includes unrealized gains/losses"
  severity: high
  frequency: 3
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Failure Propagation Edges.} The Graph Constructor then links these anti-patterns to all the downstream elements they might affect. And this is where things get interesting. It is not just the directly confused concept that gets flagged. Every formula that depends on that concept also gets connected, because if the underlying entity is wrong, any calculation built on top of it will be wrong too:
\begin{equation}
\text{affected}(a) = \{f : (a, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E \land (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, c) \in E\}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Cascade Detection.} Before GSAM even attempts to execute a new task involving some formula $f$, it first checks whether there are any upstream anti-patterns lurking in the dependency chain. It does this by traversing the dependency edges:
\begin{equation}
\text{risks}(f) = \{a : \exists\, c\ \text{s.t.}\ (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, c) \in E \land (a, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E\}
\end{equation}
When risks are found, they get surfaced to the Generator as explicit warnings. The idea is to catch potential errors before they happen, rather than cleaning up after the fact. This is really the core mechanism behind hypothesis H2: by making failure cascades visible in the graph structure, the system gains the ability to prevent errors preemptively.

\subsection{Graph Construction Pipeline}
\label{sec:construction}

GSAM's graph construction extends ACE's Curator with a Graph Constructor module that converts natural-language delta insights into graph operations. Algorithm~\ref{alg:construction} presents the complete pipeline.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{GSAM Graph Construction Pipeline}
\label{alg:construction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Task $t_i$, trajectory $\tau_i$, feedback $f_i$, current graph $G$
\STATE $\text{insights} \leftarrow \text{Reflector}(\tau_i, f_i, G)$
\STATE $\text{deltas} \leftarrow \text{Curator}(\text{insights}, G)$
\STATE $\text{operations} \leftarrow \text{GraphConstructor}(\text{deltas})$
\FOR{each operation $\text{op}$ in operations}
    \IF{op.type = \texttt{ADD\_NODE}}
        \STATE $v_{\text{new}} \leftarrow \text{CreateNode}(\text{op.node\_type}, \text{op.content})$
        \IF{$\exists\, v \in V : \text{sim}(v, v_{\text{new}}) > \theta_{\text{merge}} \land \tau_V(v) = \tau_V(v_{\text{new}})$}
            \STATE $\text{MergeNodes}(v, v_{\text{new}})$ \COMMENT{De-duplication via semantic similarity}
        \ELSE
            \STATE $V \leftarrow V \cup \{v_{\text{new}}\}$
        \ENDIF
    \ELSIF{op.type = \texttt{ADD\_EDGE}}
        \STATE $E \leftarrow E \cup \{(\text{op.source}, \text{op.relation}, \text{op.target})\}$
    \ELSIF{op.type = \texttt{UPDATE\_ATTR}}
        \STATE Update $\alpha(\text{op.node})$ with op.updates \COMMENT{Increment counters}
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $G \leftarrow \text{Prune}(G, \theta_{\text{prune}})$ \COMMENT{Remove low-utility nodes}
\RETURN $G$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Graph Constructor uses a structured LLM prompt with few-shot examples from the financial domain (see Appendix~\ref{app:prompt}) to extract entities and relationships from the Curator's natural-language deltas. Extracted nodes are de-duplicated against existing graph nodes using cosine similarity with threshold $\theta_{\text{merge}} = 0.9$, requiring matching node types. When a new node is sufficiently similar to an existing node of the same type, the two are merged: their content is combined and their counters are aggregated.

\textbf{Ontology Anchoring.} When a \textsc{Strategy} or \textsc{AntiPattern} mentions a domain concept, the Graph Constructor creates \texttt{applies\_to} or \texttt{fails\_for} edges linking to the corresponding \textsc{Concept} node in the ontology. This anchoring connects experiential knowledge to the structural backbone.

\textbf{Pruning.} Nodes with degree less than 2 and confidence below $\theta_{\text{prune}}$ for more than $K$ epochs are removed along with their incident edges.

\subsection{Multi-Epoch Graph Refinement}
\label{sec:multiepoch}

ACE supports multi-epoch adaptation, where the same training queries are revisited across multiple passes. In ACE's ablation study, removing multi-epoch reduced accuracy substantially \citep{ace2025}, confirming that second-pass encounters refine incomplete strategies.

GSAM inherits multi-epoch support but introduces \textit{graph-specific refinement operations} that go beyond what is possible with flat bullets. In flat storage, a second encounter with query $q$ can only add new bullets or increment counters. In GSAM, a second encounter triggers three additional operations:

\textbf{Edge Discovery.} On the first encounter, the Graph Constructor may create a \textsc{Strategy} node linked to the most salient \textsc{Concept}. On a second encounter with a related query, the system may discover that the strategy also applies to additional concepts, creating new \texttt{applies\_to} edges that were invisible on the first pass.

\textbf{Edge Weight Reinforcement.} When a strategy is reused successfully, the weight on its \texttt{applies\_to} edge is incremented. When it fails in a specific context, the corresponding \texttt{fails\_for} edge is created or strengthened:
\begin{equation}
w(s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) \leftarrow w(s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) + \mathbb{1}[\text{helpful in context } c]
\end{equation}

\textbf{Cross-Node Consolidation.} After each epoch, a consolidation pass identifies nodes that have acquired similar neighborhoods (connected to the same concepts via the same edge types) and merges them, producing more general strategies than would emerge from any single encounter.

What all of this amounts to is that multi-epoch adaptation in GSAM does more than just refine the \textit{content} of individual nodes, which is what ACE already does. It also refines the \textit{structure} of the graph itself. Over successive passes, the system discovers relationships that were not apparent the first time around, validates connections that seemed tentative, and merges bits of knowledge that turn out to be redundant. In a sense, the graph learns not just what to remember, but how its memories relate to one another.

\subsection{Ontology-Aware Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

When a new task $t_i = (c_i, q_i, y_i)$ comes in, GSAM needs to pull together a relevant subgraph $G_i$. This happens through three stages, each building on the last.

\textbf{Stage 1: Concept Identification.} The first step is figuring out which domain concepts actually show up in the query $q_i$ and its surrounding context $c_i$. GSAM does this by combining straightforward keyword matching against known concept names with embedding-based similarity. If something looks like a concept or semantically resembles one, it gets flagged:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_i = \{v \in V_{\textsc{Concept}} : \text{sim}(q_i \oplus c_i,\ v) > \theta_{\text{concept}}\}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 2: Graph Traversal.} Once we have a set of matched concepts $\mathcal{C}_i$, the system walks outward from them using breadth-first search, going up to depth $d = 2$. Along the way, it gathers strategies that apply to those concepts, anti-patterns that have failed for them, and any dependencies those strategies rely on:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{S}_i &= \{v : (v, \texttt{applies\_to}, c) \in E,\ c \in \mathcal{C}_i\} & \text{(strategies)} \\
\mathcal{A}_i &= \{v : (v, \texttt{fails\_for}, c) \in E,\ c \in \mathcal{C}_i\} & \text{(anti-patterns)} \\
\mathcal{D}_i &= \{v : (f, \texttt{depends\_on}, v) \in E,\ f \in \mathcal{S}_i\} & \text{(dependencies)}
\end{align}

\textbf{Stage 3: Taxonomic Expansion.} Here is where the ontology really earns its keep. For each matched concept, GSAM also pulls in strategies from sibling concepts, those that share the same parent in the taxonomy. The intuition is simple: if a strategy works for \texttt{Revenue}, it might also help with \texttt{OtherIncome}, since both live under the same branch of the XBRL tree:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}_i^+ = \mathcal{S}_i \cup \{s : (s, \texttt{applies\_to}, c') \in E \land (c', \texttt{is\_a}, \text{parent}(c)) \in E\}
\end{equation}
These borrowed strategies come with a caveat, though. They get a reduced confidence weight and are flagged as tentative, since we cannot be certain they will transfer cleanly.

\textbf{Serialization.} Finally, the retrieved subgraph has to be turned into something the Generator can actually use. GSAM serializes it into structured natural language, organizing everything by concept. Each concept's strategies, anti-patterns, and formula dependencies are grouped together, followed by a separate section listing any transferred strategies from sibling concepts. The result is a concept-centered view of all relevant knowledge, not just a flat list of bullets ranked by embedding similarity.

\section{FiNER-Transfer: A Cross-Concept Transfer Benchmark}
\label{sec:finer-transfer}
% =============================================================================

The whole point of hypothesis H1 is cross-concept transfer, and to test it properly, I needed a benchmark that actually measures transfer rather than just overall accuracy. So I built \textbf{FiNER-Transfer}, a controlled benchmark that draws directly from the XBRL taxonomy structure. Most existing benchmarks tell you how well a system performs on a task, but they do not tell you whether knowledge picked up for one concept helps, or hurts, when the system encounters a related concept. That gap is what FiNER-Transfer is meant to fill.

\subsection{Benchmark Construction}

Building FiNER-Transfer involved four steps, each designed to isolate cross-concept transfer as cleanly as possible.

\textbf{Step 1: Taxonomy Clustering.} I started by grouping the 139 FiNER entity types according to where they sit in the XBRL hierarchy. Sibling concepts, meaning those that share a direct parent, get paired together. Working through the taxonomy this way, I ended up with 42 such concept pairs.

\textbf{Step 2: Task Pair Selection.} For each pair $(c_1, c_2)$, I pulled examples from the FiNER dataset that involve each concept. The \textit{source set} contains examples for $c_1$, while the \textit{target set} contains examples for $c_2$. This gives us a clear before-and-after structure for measuring transfer.

\textbf{Step 3: Transfer Protocol.} The system first adapts on the source examples, learning whatever strategies it can for $c_1$. Then it gets evaluated on the target examples for $c_2$, but crucially, \textit{without any additional adaptation}. The transfer gain is simply the difference in accuracy:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\text{transfer}}(c_1 \rightarrow c_2) = \text{Acc}(c_2 \mid \text{adapted on } c_1) - \text{Acc}(c_2 \mid \text{no adaptation})
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 4: Controls.} Of course, not all concept pairs should transfer. To check that we are not just seeing random noise, I also included \textit{distant pairs}, concepts that live in entirely different subtrees of the taxonomy. A Balance Sheet concept paired with an Income Statement concept, for instance, should show minimal transfer. These distant pairs serve as negative controls.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

FiNER-Transfer tracks four metrics, each capturing a different aspect of transfer behavior. \textbf{Near-Transfer Rate} measures what fraction of sibling pairs show positive transfer, which is the core question the benchmark is designed to answer. \textbf{Far-Transfer Rate} does the same for distant pairs, where we expect transfer to be minimal or absent. \textbf{Transfer Precision} gives the average $\Delta_{\text{transfer}}$ among pairs that do show positive transfer, telling us how strong the transfer effect is when it happens. Finally, \textbf{Negative Transfer Rate} captures the fraction of pairs where adaptation actually \textit{hurts} performance on the target concept, a failure mode that is easy to overlook but can be quite damaging in practice.

\section{Experimental Design and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{FiNER} \citep{loukas2022finer}: This dataset focuses on financial entity recognition across 139 XBRL entity types. It includes 1,000 training examples and 300 test examples, with exact-match accuracy as the evaluation metric.

\textbf{Formula} \citep{wang2025finlora}: A numerical reasoning benchmark built on XBRL filings. The dataset has 800 training and 200 test examples. Again, exact-match accuracy is the metric.

\textbf{FiNER-Transfer} (introduced in this thesis): The benchmark I constructed for measuring cross-concept transfer. It consists of 42 sibling concept pairs drawn from the XBRL taxonomy, along with 42 distant pairs that serve as controls.

\subsection{Baselines and Methods}

Every method in these experiments runs on DeepSeek-V3.1 \citep{deepseek2024} as the base LLM, which keeps things consistent with how ACE was originally evaluated. The baselines I compare against are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Base LLM}: The model evaluated directly, with no context engineering at all.
\item \textbf{ICL} \citep{brown2020language}: Standard in-context learning, where training examples are randomly sampled and included in the prompt.
\item \textbf{GEPA} \citep{agrawal2025gepa}: Genetic Pareto prompt optimization. This approach uses evolutionary strategies to search over different prompt variants.
\item \textbf{ACE} \citep{ace2025}: Agentic context engineering with flat bullet storage. This is the main baseline GSAM is compared against, since ACE represents the current state of the art for context engineering without graph structure.
\item \textbf{GSAM} (this thesis): Graph-structured adaptive memory with ontology grounding, the system I am proposing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

I follow ACE's evaluation protocol to ensure fair comparison:

\textbf{Offline Adaptation.} Methods are optimized on the training split across 5 epochs and evaluated on the test split. For GSAM, the ontology is initialized before training, and the graph grows during adaptation.

\textbf{Online Adaptation.} Methods are evaluated sequentially on the test split: for each sample, the model first predicts with the current knowledge, then updates its memory. The same shuffled test order is used across all methods.

\textbf{Offline Warmup + Online.} Following ACE's strongest configuration, GSAM is also evaluated with an offline warmup phase (5 epochs on training data) followed by online adaptation on the test split.

The reason is straightforward: BFS from matched concept nodes only visits the relevant subgraph, whereas ACE has to scan all embeddings every time. That difference compounds quickly as knowledge accumulates.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{When Does Graph Structure Help Most?}

Looking at the results, three scenarios stand out where GSAM really pulls ahead of flat storage:

\textbf{High Domain Structure.} When a domain comes with a formal ontology, things like XBRL in finance, ICD codes in medicine, or Gene Ontology in biology, GSAM can lean on that ground-truth taxonomic structure for both organizing knowledge and enabling cross-concept transfer. Now, in domains that lack such formal ontologies, GSAM can still learn structure from the data itself. But the advantage over flat storage shrinks in those cases. The richer the pre-existing structure, the more GSAM has to work with.

\textbf{Compositional Tasks.} Tasks where getting the right answer depends on multiple interconnected concepts benefit most from failure cascade modeling. Formula tasks are a good example: they require correctly extracting several entities, and if you get one wrong, it cascades into the numerical reasoning. The cascade mechanism helps here by surfacing warnings about upstream failures before they ripple into downstream computations.

\textbf{Large Knowledge Bases.} Once accumulated knowledge grows beyond roughly 200 items, flat retrieval precision starts to degrade because the embedding space gets crowded. Graph-based filtering sidesteps this problem by restricting retrieval to structurally relevant subsets, keeping precision high even as the knowledge base expands.

That said, for simple, single-concept tasks with fewer than 50 accumulated knowledge items, ACE's flat bullets are probably fine. In those cases, GSAM's added complexity is not really justified.

\subsection{Limitations}

No system is without its shortcomings, and GSAM is no exception. Several limitations should be acknowledged.

\textbf{Extraction Quality.} The Graph Constructor depends on an LLM to pull out entities and relationships from natural-language insights. If the extraction goes wrong, those errors end up in the graph, potentially creating spurious nodes or bogus edges. Structured prompts and de-duplication help, but they do not eliminate the problem entirely. A more systematic evaluation of extraction quality would be valuable future work.

\textbf{Schema Generality.} The node and edge types in Table~\ref{tab:schema} are tailored for financial analysis. Other domains would need different schemas. Medical reasoning, for instance, might call for \textsc{Symptom}, \textsc{Diagnosis}, and \textsc{Treatment} node types, with edges like \texttt{indicates} and \texttt{contraindicated\_with}. Figuring out principled methods for automatic schema adaptation remains an open problem.

\textbf{Scalability.} The experiments here involve graphs with hundreds to low thousands of nodes. Domains that accumulate tens of thousands of experiences over extended deployment might run into trouble. At that scale, you would probably need hierarchical graph structures, some form of graph summarization, or approximate traversal algorithms.

\textbf{Single-Domain Evaluation.} Financial analysis was chosen for good reasons: it has a formal ontology and natural dependencies between concepts. Still, demonstrating GSAM's benefits across multiple domains would make the generality claims more convincing.

\subsection{Comparison with Other Memory Architectures}

GSAM occupies a specific position in the design space of agent memory systems. Relative to A-MEM's Zettelkasten-inspired approach \citep{xu2025amem}, GSAM adds formal ontology grounding and typed edges. Where ExpeL \citep{zhao2024expel} accumulates task-specific expertise, GSAM models cross-task relationships explicitly. And unlike RAG-based approaches \citep{lewis2020rag}, which retrieve from a static corpus, GSAM constructs its knowledge base dynamically from learning experiences.

The closest conceptual relative is probably QA-GNN \citep{yasunaga2021qagnn}, which also marries language models with graph-structured knowledge. The key difference is that QA-GNN relies on a pre-existing knowledge graph (ConceptNet), whereas GSAM builds its graph from scratch, grounded in a domain ontology. That distinction matters: GSAM can adapt to new domains without requiring a pre-built knowledge base, though it does need a schema to work from.
\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}
% =============================================================================

This thesis introduced Graph-Structured Adaptive Memory (GSAM), a framework that replaces the flat bullet-point storage used by context adaptation systems like ACE with an ontology-grounded knowledge graph. The core insight is that in knowledge-intensive domains, the \textit{relationships between} knowledge elements are as important as the elements themselves.

\subsection{Key Findings}

The experiments on financial analysis benchmarks turned out to support all three research hypotheses, though the strength of the evidence varies.

On \textbf{H1 (Cross-Concept Transfer)}: GSAM achieved a near-transfer rate of 64.3\% on the FiNER-Transfer benchmark, compared to just 26.2\% for ACE. The XBRL taxonomy provides taxonomic edges that enable principled knowledge transfer between sibling concepts. To give a concrete example, a strategy for distinguishing \texttt{Revenue} from \texttt{OtherIncome} can transfer to distinguishing \texttt{OperatingExpenses} from \texttt{NonOperatingExpenses}, because both pairs occupy the same structural relationship in the taxonomy. Perhaps more telling, GSAM's negative transfer rate (7.1\%) was substantially lower than ACE's (19.0\%), which suggests that ontology-guided constraints actually do prevent inappropriate transfer.

On \textbf{H2 (Failure Prevention)}: GSAM reduced the repeated failure rate from 31.5\% (ACE) to 14.2\%, a 54.9\% reduction. The failure cascade model explicitly links anti-patterns to affected downstream elements, so once an error pattern gets identified, warnings propagate to all tasks that could be affected. This mechanism proved most effective for Formula tasks, where entity extraction errors tend to cascade into numerical reasoning errors.

On \textbf{H3 (Retrieval Precision)}: GSAM's retrieval precision (73.8\%) was substantially higher than ACE's (42.3\%). By combining concept identification with graph traversal, GSAM retrieves knowledge that is \textit{structurally} relevant rather than merely \textit{textually} similar. That distinction matters: it reduces prompt noise and improves Generator performance.

Overall task accuracy improved modestly but consistently: +2.4\% on FiNER and +3.1\% on Formula (averaging offline and online settings). The gains were larger on Formula, which fits with the hypothesis that failure cascade modeling has the greatest impact on compositional tasks.

\subsection{Contributions}

This thesis makes several contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The GSAM framework} itself, with its knowledge graph schema, Graph Constructor, and Graph Retriever components that extend the ACE architecture.
\item \textbf{The Domain Ontology Layer}, showing that pre-existing domain structure (in this case, the XBRL taxonomy) can be leveraged to organize learned knowledge and enable cross-concept transfer.
\item \textbf{The Failure Cascade Model}, demonstrating that modeling error propagation as explicit graph edges reduces repeated and downstream failures.
\item \textbf{The FiNER-Transfer benchmark}, which offers a controlled methodology for evaluating cross-concept knowledge transfer within a domain taxonomy.
\item \textbf{A thorough analysis} of the latency, cost, and scalability trade-offs of graph-structured memory compared to flat storage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}

There are several directions worth exploring:

\textbf{Graph Neural Networks for Retrieval.} Replacing BFS traversal with learned message-passing models (such as graph attention networks) could weight edges by task-specific relevance. This might improve retrieval precision further, though it would add training overhead.

\textbf{Multi-Domain Graphs.} Connecting financial, legal, and regulatory knowledge in a single graph could support complex compliance tasks that span domain boundaries. Whether the added complexity is worth it depends on the use case.

\textbf{Collaborative Graphs.} Allowing multiple LLM agents to share and contribute to a common knowledge graph could enable collective knowledge accumulation, somewhat analogous to how human teams share institutional knowledge.

\textbf{Integration with Fine-Tuning.} Using the graph structure to decide which knowledge should remain in the context (frequently accessed, high-variance items) versus which should be distilled into model weights (stable, well-validated strategies) could help optimize the boundary between context adaptation and parameter updates.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}

GSAM is implemented as an extension of the ACE codebase (\url{https://github.com/ace-agent/ace}). The key new modules are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{gsam/graph\_memory.py}: NetworkX-based knowledge graph with typed nodes and edges, supporting add, merge, prune, retrieve, and serialize operations.
\item \texttt{gsam/ontology.py}: XBRL taxonomy parser that initializes \textsc{Concept} nodes and \texttt{is\_a} edges from the taxonomy hierarchy.
\item \texttt{gsam/core/graph\_constructor.py}: LLM-based entity and relationship extraction from Curator deltas.
\item \texttt{gsam/core/graph\_retriever.py}: Three-stage retrieval: concept identification, graph BFS traversal, and taxonomic expansion.
\item \texttt{finance/run\_gsam.py}: Entry point for GSAM experiments on financial benchmarks.
\end{itemize}

For all experiments, we ran DeepSeek-V3.1 through the SambaNova API, capping each LLM call at 4,096 tokens. To measure node similarity, we relied on \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} from the sentence-transformers library, which struck a reasonable balance between speed and quality. We set the de-duplication threshold at $\theta_{\text{merge}} = 0.9$, though in hindsight a slightly lower value might capture more borderline duplicates. Retrieval uses a BFS traversal with depth $d = 2$, which tends to surface relevant context without pulling in too much noise.
